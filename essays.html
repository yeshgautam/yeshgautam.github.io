NYT Summer Reading Contest · technology
AI as a Catalyst, Not a Replacement
Persuasive essay, 2026 · responding to “Forget Coders. The Real A.I. Threat Is in the Back Office.”
AI has started replacing jobs like customer service representatives and payroll clerks according to the New York Times article “The Hidden Workers Most Threatened by AI,” which warns of “lasting consequences” if we take AI too far. However I take a more positive view: humans are one of the most adaptable species on this planet. We have adapted well to harsh conditions and crises throughout history. Humanity feared electricity because of how unpredictable and how unnatural it felt as people watched Edison publicly electrocuting animals to prove electrical currents were dangerous. History has shown that when crisis hits, humans don't only survive, they evolve. AI may be considered a “crisis” by some now, but I believe it can be harnessed by the human intellect to drive innovation in fields like medicine. For example in the field of cancer detection, Google's DeepMind, a research lab bought by Google, has developed AI that detects breast and lung cancer earlier than trained radiologists. AI is forcing humanity to automate repetitive tasks, which as a pro is freeing up time for us to focus on judgment-intensive work. Ultimately, we should follow the lead of companies like IKEA who have embraced AI by letting it do repetitive roles while keeping jobs intact. We should use AI not as a replacement for jobs but as a catalyst for new ones.
NYT Summer Reading Contest · economics
China's EV Strategy
Persuasive essay, 2026 · responding to “China's Edge in an Oil Shock: Electric Cars and Renewables”
While soaring oil prices have sent the world scrambling, for $100 China has barely flinched. According to “China's Edge in an Oil Shock: Electric Cars and Renewables” China has been preparing for decades. In 2004, China saw how dependent they were on the Strait of Malacca, as a Chinese state newspaper stated “Whoever controls [the Strait of Malacca] will have a stranglehold on China's energy.” Beijing invested 300 billion dollars into electric vehicles and renewables, backing companies like BYD, named ‘world's top electric vehicle seller’ financially as they overtook western companies like Tesla. China over the years has invested in public transit systems which connect 90% of urban areas and is very affordable. While China was building for oil replacement, the US was giving oil companies billions in tax breaks. Rather than eliminating tax breaks and risking price rises, those breaks should come with a mandated percentage to be reinvested into EVs and renewable infrastructure, letting oil gradually fund its own replacement. The vehicle is only half the problem. Most American cities are designed around vehicles. In the suburbs, a car is a necessity. The US needs to invest in infrastructure. Connecting cities with transit will be costly upfront, but it will save the US in the long run. China didn't get lucky, they were building for the future. While the world is scrambling, China is simply driving: no gas, no panic, no problem. The path forward exists, the US just needs to take it.
NYT Summer Reading Contest · policy
The Case for City-Owned Grocery Stores
Persuasive essay, 2026 · responding to “Mamdani Plans to Open City-Owned Grocery Store in East Harlem”
New York City's grocery prices have soared high, and Mayor Mamdani thinks taxpayers money can fix it. The New York Times article “Mamdani Plans to Open City-Owned Grocery Store in East Harlem” lays out Mamdani's $30 million proposal to bring affordable groceries to New York City. Critics like John Catsimatidis, a supermarket chain owner, have dismissed the plan because this would cause competition, which would force him to match or lower prices. But these criticisms overlook the bigger picture. One of the US's biggest problems has been its inability to control necessities such as housing, healthcare, and food. The government already runs a complicated system like postal services that move millions of letters across the country every day. Running a handful of grocery stores in New York City given its $110 billion budget is not too much to ask for, especially when the stakes are families being able to put food on the table without sacrificing something else valuable to them. Skyrocketing grocery prices are a nationwide problem, stretching from urban towns to rural neighborhoods. If New York City's model proves to be successful then the US federal government should take note and open city-owned grocery stores around the US, making basic food necessities like eggs, bread, and vegetables affordable for every resident. If this works for New York City, this can determine how the US approaches food insecurity as a national issue rather than a local inconvenience.
